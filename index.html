About Me

A blamer

In high school, I used to look down on people and complain a lot but never actually did anything about it. I thought everything around me was subpar and that I was better than my surroundings, but I didn't show it because I felt the environment didn't support it (which was just an excuse).

Then I met a senior who was two years older than me, also in the gifted program. Unlike me, he didn't let the environment hold him back. I looked him up and found out he had published research before even graduating high school, which was amazing to me. His work was pure engineering—mechanical, electrical, programming, data analysis—all coming together. It was like something adults would do. It totally opened my eyes.

I was confused. How could he do so much in the same environment I thought wasn't conducive? He didn't wait—he just started it himself. How could he work on projects that seemed beyond high school level and make them look so polished? Plus, he also co-founded the Space AC Institute of Technology.

Getting to know him literally halved my ego and reminded me that if I left my excuses behind, many doors of opportunity would open in front of me.

Mental breakdown

A few years back, my mental health was really bad—I was crying and doubting myself every day. I tried everything: more sleep, hanging out more, doing fun stuff, but what really turned things around was stumbling upon stoicism.

Stoicism is straightforward yet super effective. It says a lot of our bad feelings come from trying to control stuff that's just too hard or impossible to control. So, the trick is to focus on what you can control (you decide what that is) and forget about the rest. It's like dealing with gravity—you accept what you can't change and figure out how to handle it.

It sounds simple, but making it work takes practice. Whenever I hit a mental roadblock these days, I go back to my favorite stoic book and do a few exercises from it. I treat it like my personal holy bible for coping.

I believe in giving

Deals work best when both sides feel like they're getting more than they're giving. So, to win in any circumstance, we need to give a lot.

By giving a lot to others, they will share their knowledge with us too. This back-and-forth can build a community of people who love sharing and learning.

It's totally fine to share as a beginner; just be honest about your level.

"Fake it until you make it" means to act like your ideal self at your current level, not pretending to be an expert if you're not.

Their manifesto page really inspired me. Every field has its own toolbox and set of tools. So when experts from different fields come together and aren't bothered by each other's ways of solving problems, some really cool things can happen.

The passion tradeoff

I've seen endless debates on social media about specialists versus generalists. It seems like most people root for the generalists. But after thinking it over and over, I've realized there's no need to label yourself as either.

The key is to stay curious and keep learning. With that principle alone, we can handle whatever life throws at us.

But recently, I've learned the hard way that working harder doesn't always solve everything. There's a tradeoff between hard work and happiness. No matter how passionate you are, nobody wants to grind 24/7. Everyone has their own balance between work and happiness.

So, we have to carefully choose what works best for us. It sounds simple until your personal life starts falling apart, like mine did. That's when I realized I needed to take action—work less—before everything collapsed.
